## Course Description

Prepares students for internships and entry-level roles in software development. Focus will be on how to manage the internship/career search and how to effectively secure a position. Personal branding, technical resumes, and industry-specific interviewing skills will also be covered. Previously IT 378.

## Learning Outcomes

The following course learning outcomes, program-learning outcomes, and campus-wide learning outcomes will be assessed by the course assignments and exams.

### Course Outcomes

Students who successfully complete this class will be able to:

- Demonstrate individual responsibility by taking ownership of their internship and career search.
- Recognize the major elements of the technical career search that are within one’s own control.
- Prepare for technical career specific elements of the career search such as one-way video interviews, whiteboarding interviews, and the STAR method of answering behavioral interview questions.
- Set goals, plan, and track progress in the career search and career development processes.
- Create a technical resume that is targeted for a specific technical role.
- Develop a personal brand and the artifacts to support and communicate the brand to others.
- Establish a professional network, online and in-person, leveraging existing resources, to support the career search and development process.

### Program Outcomes

4. Perform software quality assurance activities throughout the entire software lifecycle.

## Statement of Instructional Methods

This course uses a studio and lab-based learning model. The majority of class time is dedicated to hands-on project work, pair programming, and collaborative problem-solving. The instructor serves as a technical mentor. They will circulate during lab time, meet regularly with teams and individuals, and provide direct feedback on student work.

A typical class session includes a short instructor-led segment covering key concepts, common pitfalls, or a demonstration and is followed by extended collaborative work time. Activities may include pair programming exercises, guided experiments with AI models, peer discussion, and troubleshooting sessions.

Students are expected to complete preparatory materials (videos, readings, tutorials, and Microsoft Learn modules) before class so that class time can focus on applied work. Prep materials are provided on Canvas and are not graded, but students who come to class unprepared will find it difficult to participate meaningfully in that session's activities.

Regular in-person attendance is **essential**. This course is built around collaborative, in-class work that cannot be replicated asynchronously. Your grade is primarily determined by consistent participation in the learning activities that happen in the room.

## Modality Statement

This is a hybrid course that meets live, in-person four hours per week and includes an asynchronous online component in Canvas. The in-person sessions are the core of the course. This is where project work, pair programming, team collaboration, and instructor feedback happen. The Canvas component is used for prep materials, announcements, and submitting work artifacts (reflections, repository links, Microsoft Learn progress, etc.). Regular in-person attendance is **required**. Students who frequently miss class sessions will see significant grade impacts that cannot be compensated through asynchronous work alone.

## Academic Honesty

All code you submit must represent work you understand and can explain. This is the standard that matters in this course. Please be aware that other courses may have different policies concerning academic honesty and AI use.

- **AI Use:** AI tools (ChatGPT, Copilot, Claude, etc.) are permitted in this class. These are real tools used in industry and learning to work with them effectively is a legitimate skill. However, AI should support your understanding, not replace it. If you submit work you cannot explain, that is a problem because you do not understand your own code. During check-ins and conversations about your work during the quarter, I will ask you questions about what you built and how it works. If it becomes clear that you do not understand the code you submitted, we will have a conversation about it. Repeated inability to explain your own work may be treated as academic dishonesty and can result in a zero for the assignment(s) in question.
- **Collaboration:** This course is built around collaboration. You will pair program, work in teams, and discuss ideas regularly. This is expected and encouraged. If you adapt code from a classmate, a website, or any other source, cite it. The line between collaboration and plagiarism is straightforward: can you explain what the code does and why? If yes, you are fine. If no, there is a problem regardless of where the code came from.
- **Plagiarism:** Submitting someone else's work as your own, having someone else complete assignments for you, or submitting work you cannot explain constitutes academic dishonesty and will result in a zero for that assignment.

## Assessment and Grading

This course uses a **completion-based grading system**. There are no points, rubrics, or quality scores on individual assignments. Every graded item is assessed as **complete** (full credit) or **incomplete** (zero). Your final grade is determined by the proportion of items you complete across the quarter. All items are worth one point.

A student who attends consistently, participates in class activities, and submits deliverables will earn an A. Missed items lower your grade proportionally. Canvas calculates your grade automatically from your completion record. Your three lowest attendance scores are dropped automatically.
